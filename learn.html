Basics of Fire Safety

Here we will learn about the basics of fire safety one should know before starting their training.

1. Understanding Fire Basics

The Fire Triangle

A fire needs three elements to start and sustain: Heat, Fuel, and Oxygen. Removing any one of these elements will extinguish the fire.

Classes of Fires

Fires are classified based on the type of material that is burning:

  • Class A: Ordinary combustibles such as wood, paper, cloth, and plastic.
  • Class B: Flammable liquids and gases like oil, gasoline, and propane.
  • Class C: Electrical fires involving energized electrical equipment.
  • Class D: Combustible metals such as magnesium and titanium.
  • Class K (or F): Cooking oils and fats, like vegetable or animal oils, typically found in kitchens.

Common Causes of Fires in Hotels

Be aware of potential fire hazards such as:

  • Electrical faults in wiring or appliances.
  • Cooking accidents, especially in staff kitchens or unauthorized cooking in guest rooms.
  • Unattended candles or smoking in non-smoking areas.
  • Improper storage of flammable materials like cleaning supplies.

2. Fire Extinguishers

Types of Fire Extinguishers

Different extinguishers are designed for different classes of fires:

  • Water Extinguishers: Suitable for Class A fires.
  • Foam Extinguishers: Effective on Class A and B fires.
  • Dry Chemical Extinguishers: Can be used on Class A, B, and C fires.
  • Carbon Dioxide (CO2) Extinguishers: Best for Class B and C fires, often used around electrical equipment.
  • Wet Chemical Extinguishers: Specifically designed for Class K (or F) fires in kitchens.

Identifying the Right Extinguisher

Always check the label on the fire extinguisher to ensure it is the correct type for the fire you are trying to extinguish. Look for the class of fire indicated on the label (e.g., A, B, C, K).

How to Use a Fire Extinguisher (P.A.S.S. Method)

  1. Pull the pin.
  2. Aim the nozzle at the base of the fire.
  3. Squeeze the lever slowly and evenly.
  4. Sweep the nozzle from side to side at the base of the fire until the fire is out.

Location and Inspection

Know the location of fire extinguishers on your floor and in your work areas. Regularly check that extinguishers are in their designated places and have not been tampered with.

3. Fire Alarm Systems

Types of Fire Detection

  • Smoke Detectors: These devices sense smoke in the air and trigger the alarm.
  • Heat Detectors: These activate when the temperature in a room rises rapidly or reaches a certain level.
  • Manual Pull Stations: These are located at various points and allow anyone to manually activate the fire alarm.

Recognizing and Responding to the Fire Alarm

Familiarize yourself with the sound of the hotel's fire alarm. When you hear it:

  • Investigate: If it is safe to do so, try to determine the location and nature of the potential fire.
  • Evacuate: If a fire is confirmed, or if instructed by management or the alarm system, begin the evacuation process immediately.
  • Report: Report the situation to your supervisor or call the emergency number.

    • Do Not Tamper

    Tampering with fire alarm systems is dangerous and illegal. Never disable or cover smoke detectors or pull stations.

4. Evacuation Procedures

Hotel Evacuation Plan

Every hotel has an evacuation plan. Make sure you know and understand your hotel's specific plan, including evacuation routes and assembly points.

Staff Roles During Evacuation

During an evacuation, your responsibilities may include:

  • Guiding guests towards the nearest safe exit.
  • Checking guest rooms to ensure everyone has evacuated.
  • Assisting guests who may need help, such as the elderly or those with disabilities.
  • Keeping guests calm and providing clear instructions.

Emergency Exits and Assembly Points

Know the locations of all emergency exits on each floor and the designated assembly points outside the hotel. Guide guests to these safe areas.

Assisting Guests with Disabilities

Be prepared to assist guests with disabilities according to your training. This may involve helping them to designated safe areas or providing specific instructions.

Stay Calm and Follow Instructions

It is crucial to remain calm during an evacuation. Follow the instructions of fire safety personnel and encourage guests to do the same.

Checking Rooms

If it is part of your role, quickly check guest rooms as you evacuate, knocking loudly and announcing the fire. Ensure no one is left behind.

5. Emergency Communication

Internal Communication

Understand the hotel's internal communication protocols during a fire emergency. This might involve using two-way radios or specific phone lines to report information to supervisors or the front desk.

Reporting to Emergency Services

Know the local emergency phone number (e.g., 911 or your local equivalent). When reporting a fire, provide clear and concise information, including the exact location of the fire, what is burning, and if anyone is injured.

Communicating with Guests

When communicating with guests during an emergency, speak clearly and calmly. Provide accurate information about the situation and the steps they need to take.

6. Fire Prevention

General Fire Safety Tips for Staff

  • Never leave cooking unattended.
  • Ensure electrical appliances are in good working order and not overloaded.
  • Dispose of smoking materials properly in designated containers.
  • Keep hallways and exits clear of obstructions.
  • Report any potential fire hazards to your supervisor immediately.

Specific Guidelines for Different Areas

  • Kitchen: Be vigilant during cooking, keep cooking areas clean, and know how to handle grease fires.
  • Guest Rooms: Remind guests of no-smoking policies and to be cautious with electrical devices.
  • Storage Areas: Store flammable materials properly and ensure good ventilation.

Good Housekeeping

Maintaining a clean and organized environment can significantly reduce the risk of fire. Ensure that trash is disposed of properly and that storage areas are not cluttered.

Safe Handling of Flammable Materials

If your job involves handling flammable liquids or other hazardous materials, follow the hotel's specific procedures for their safe use and storage.

7. Consequences of Incorrect Decisions

Making incorrect decisions during a fire emergency can have serious consequences, including:

  • Putting yourself and others at risk of injury or even death.
  • Allowing the fire to spread, causing more damage to the property.
  • Hindering the safe evacuation of guests and staff.

It is vital to follow established fire safety protocols and your training to ensure the safety of everyone in the hotel.

8. Hotel-Specific Fire Safety Plan

Refer to your hotel's specific fire safety manual for detailed information about emergency procedures, evacuation routes, and the location of safety equipment. Make sure you are familiar with this plan and participate actively in regular fire drills to practice these procedures.
